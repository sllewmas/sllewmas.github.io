Chromaticism: the use of pitches foreign to the key of a passage

Altered Chords: chords that use a tone from outside the key

Secondary Function: A chord whose function (i.e. Roman Numeral) belongs more closely to a key other than the main key of the passage

Altered chords are the most common sort of secondary functions

Most secondary functions are secondary dominants or secondary leading-tone chords.

V/x → x

V7/x → x

Secondary dominants can tonicize only major or minor triads (or major or minor triads with a seventh added)

Secondary Leading-Tone Chords

When we use a secondary leading-tone seventh chord we have to decide which seventh chord to use

vii°7 or viiø7

  1. If the triad to be tonicized is minor, use vii°7
  2. If the triad to be tonicized is major, use either vii°7 or viiø7
    • vii°7 is the most common choice.

Spelling Secondary Leading-Tone Chords

There are three steps involved in spelling a secondary leading-tone chord

  1. Find the root of the chord that is to be tonicized
  2. Go down a m2 (half-step).
  3. Using that note as the root, spell a DIMINISHED triad (for vii° of), a DIMINISHED SEVENTH chord (for vii°7 of), or a HALF-DIMINISHED SEVENTH chord (for viiø7).

Recognizing Secondary Leading-Tone Chords

If you find an altered chord and it is not a V(7)/, there is a good chance it will be a secondary leading-tone chord.

  1. Is the chord a diminished triad, a diminished seventh, or a half-diminished seventh chord? If not, it is not a secondary leading-tone chord.
  2. Find the note a m2 (half-step) above the root of the altered chord.
  3. Would a major of minor triad built on that note be a diatonic triad in this key? If so, the altered chord is a secondary leading-tone chord.

Deceptive Resolutions of Secondary Functions

Deceptive resolutions of secondary functions do occur.

Especially common is the resolution of a V7/ up to the vi (or VI) of the chord that was being tonicized.

for example: V7/vi → VI/vi
