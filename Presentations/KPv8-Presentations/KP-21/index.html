Mode Mixture and the Neapolitan

Today, we look at a number of chords that are used to provide color to a musical passage.

Most of them do this through mode mixture, the injecting of elements of the minor mode into a passage in major, and they do this through the use of ♭6̂, ♭3̂, and even ♭7̂.

We also discuss the Neapolitan chord, a major triad used chiefly in the minor mode and which uses ♭2̂ as its root.

Borrowed Chords in Minor

There is one chord frequently borrowed from major that contains the raised 3̂, and that chord is the major tonic triad itself. The raised 3̂ in the tonic triad is called the Picardy third, and it was used to end most compositions in minor from the early 1500s until around 1750.

Borrowed Chords in Major:

The Use of ♭6̂

  • The most frequently encountered examples of mode mixture in the major mode involve chords that employ ♭6̂
    • The ♭6̂ here refers to the lowered sixth scale degree, and does not neccesarily mean that the note has a ♭ accidental.
  • Borrowing ♭6̂ from the parallel minor creates four borrowed chords that are frequently used in major:

vii°7, ii°, iiø7, and iv

Other Borrowed Chords in Major

  • The most frequently encountered examples of mode mixture in major are those chords that “borrow” only ♭6̂
  • vii°7, ii°, iiø7, and iv
  • The next most common examples of mode mixture make use of ♭3̂
  • i, ♭VI, iv7,
  • Least common are those that use ♭7̂
  • ♭III, and ♭VIII

The Neapolitan Chord

  • Overall, harmony is organized by the I-V-I progression.
  • Looking at the big picture: We work towards V and V takes us back to I.
  • While this is an extemely effective shape for a piece, foreground harmonic interest in a tonal passage may be provided by the ways in which the dominant is approached.
  • One of the more colorful chords that can be used to precede the dominant is the Neapolitan.

The Neapolitan Chord

  • The Neapolitan chord derives its name from an important group of eighteenth-century opera composers who were associated with the city of Naples.
  • They did not invent it, but they used it a bunch, and the name stuck.
  • The Neapolitan triad is a major triad constructed on the lowered second scale degree.
  • We label with: N or ♭II.

The Neapolitan Chord

  • We so often see the Neapolitan chord in first inversion, that is often called the Neapolitan sixth chord
  • N6
  • Borrowed chords and the Neapolitan can serve as the common chord in modulations, often facilitating the movement to a foreign key.
